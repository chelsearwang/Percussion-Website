Snare Drum

The snare drum is a versatile and essential percussion instrument. It produces a sharp and crisp sound and is typically played with drumsticks. Known for its distinctive snare wires, the instrument can create both rhythmic patterns and dynamic accents. The snare drum contributes untuned notes to the percussion ensemble.

Bass Drum

As the powerhouse of percussion, the bass drum produces deep, resonant, and thunderous sounds. Played with mallets or beaters, it adds weight and impact to the rhythmic foundation of music. The bass drum contributes untuned, non-pitched notes to the ensemble.

Marimba

A mallet percussion instrument, the marimba features wooden bars arranged like a keyboard. Played with yarn mallets, it produces resonant and melodic tones. The marimba distinguishes itself with its larger, resonant tubes, creating a warm and rich sound.

Xylophone

Similar to the marimba, the xylophone is a mallet percussion instrument with wooden bars. It produces bright and percussive tones when struck with mallets, usually made of plastic. The xylophone has a distinct, crisp sound and is usually smaller than the marimba.

Vibraphone

The vibraphone is a mallet instrument with metal bars, resonators, and a pedal. It is usually played with yarn mallets harder than those used for a marimba, and it produces a warm, shimmering sound. What sets the vibraphone apart is its motor-driven rotating discs in the resonators, creating a vibrato effect that adds a unique dimension to its sound. It can also be bowed to create a unique and etheal sound.

Glockenspiel / Orchestra Bells

Bells, in the percussion context, refer to tuned metal bars played with mallets. Producing clear and bright tones, bells contribute a melodic element to the ensemble. Orchestral bells sometimes have a pedal, just like the vibraphone. However, not all bells do, such as marching bells used in marching band. Bells are played with hard mallets made of plastic or metal.

Timpani

The timpani, often referred to as kettle drums, play a pivotal role in the percussion family. These sizable drums produce a commanding and resonant sound, and their pitch can be adjusted by manipulating the tension of the drumheads. Employing padded mallets, the timpanist, who typically manages drums of various sizes, introduces melodic elements and dynamic accents to musical compositions. This melodic versatility adds a distinctive layer to the rhythmic foundation, contributing richness and grandeur to orchestral and ensemble performances.

Triangle

The triangle is a small, untuned percussion instrument played with a metal beater. Despite its simple design, it produces a bright, shimmering sound. Typically used for accents and color in the ensemble, the triangle contributes untuned, non-pitched notes.

Tambourine

The tambourine is a handheld instrument with jingling metal disks or small cymbals attached to its frame. Played by shaking or striking, it produces a rhythmic and lively sound, adding texture and excitement to the ensemble. The tambourine contributes untuned, non-pitched notes.

Concert Toms

Concert toms are a set of untuned drums that vary in size and pitch. Played with drumsticks, they contribute a punchy and resonant sound to the percussion ensemble. Unlike traditional toms on a drum set, concert toms are often standalone drums without bottom heads, producing a clear and focused tone.
